# Forbidden Spice — Blurb, Foreword & Afterword
*First Drafts — Silver Wizard Publishing*

---

## BLURB

She wore turquoise lace and told him to close his eyes.

He was seventy-three. She was twenty-five. The Adriatic glittered beyond the cabin glass, and for fifteen minutes, nothing was complicated.

*Forbidden Spice* opens at the edge of everything falling apart — then travels back forty months through cities across Europe and the fullest telling of an impossible love affair: two people who should never have worked, and then did, brilliantly and disastrously, and kept doing it anyway.

Mark is retired, biohacked back to vitality, and certain he has made Sofia the goddess she is becoming. Sofia knows exactly who she is becoming — and is not so sure she owes it to him.

Told in two voices — Mark's rendered desire and Sofia's razor-sharp interior counterpoint — *Forbidden Spice* is autofiction about the oldest question: what happens when you fully awaken someone? What do they do with what they've learned? And does the person who lit the match get to claim the fire?

It is explicit. It is unflinching. And the ending — which Mark imagined before he understood that reality would be stranger — is devastating.

He always told her she could command any room. Win any heart.

She believed him. Then she stayed.

*A Novel*

## FOREWORD

*Mark Harde*

The first thing to say is that most of this happened.

The cities are real — Lisbon, Paphos, Budapest, Ayia Napa, Granada, and finally the Adriatic on a ship we both now refer to as the cruise from hell. The forty-eight-year age gap is real. The dual voice is real: she read every [Sofia] section, amended some, added others I had no way of knowing. The desire, the jealousy, the shame — all real, in both directions.

What is not real is the ending.

In the final pages of this novel, I wrote an ending that frightened me more than anything else in the book — an ending I spent forty months being afraid of. A man in white linen. A message at 2:07 in the morning. Sofia gone at dawn. I wrote it because I needed to understand what we were risking. Because the threat was real, even if the event was not. Because autofiction earns its name: it borrows the form of fact and uses it to tell the truth about fear.

As I write this, she is asleep two feet away.

That is the real ending. It is also, in its way, more unlikely than anything I invented.

Here is what I would have you understand before you begin: I am seventy-three years old. I have been young, I have been invisible, and I have been — briefly and inexplicably — returned to a version of myself that wanted everything. Sofia was twenty-five during the events of this book, and arriving at her power at the precise moment I was arriving at what I thought was peace. We collided with the specific force of two people who have each decided, separately, that they have nothing left to lose.

I was wrong about that. So was she, in different ways.

People will have opinions about what we are to each other. About the age gap, the money, the desire itself. Some of those opinions will be right. I have tried, in writing this, to see what we are from the outside as clearly as I could, while still being helplessly the man inside it.

What I know is this: the ending I wrote is a confession of what I feared losing. The ending that actually happened is a thing I don't quite have the words for yet.

Give me another forty months.

*— Mark Harde*
*Lisbon, 2025*

## AFTERWORD

*Sofia*

He showed me the ending before he finished it. I read it on his phone while he pretended not to watch my face.

I said: *this isn't what happened.*

He said: *I know.*

I said: *so why did you write it?*

He thought about it for a moment. Then he said: *because for most of the last three years, it's what I expected to happen. And I needed to write it out of me.*

I understood that. I didn't like it, but I understood it.

What actually happened was smaller, more ordinary, and — given what you've just read — probably more surprising. We had a week apart. We found our way back. The cruise ended the way cruises end: with too much luggage, a long flight, and the specific relief of solid ground.

He is not always easy to love. The possessiveness is real. The fear of losing me is real and it makes him act in ways that cost us both. The age gap costs us in ways neither of us fully predicted. I have an entire interior life in the gaps between [Sofia] sections that this book cannot contain.

But the thing he wrote about in the prologue — his ability to pay attention, to learn, to understand that my body wasn't a puzzle to solve but a language to speak — that is also real. It remains real. It is still the thing I haven't found anywhere else, and I have looked.

He made me more of what I was. That is the truest thing in this book.

The ending he invented is what he feared. The fact that I'm writing this — that he asked me to, and that I agreed — is what actually happened. Make of that what you will.

I'm still here.

*— Sofia*
*Lisbon, 2025*

*PIC editorial note — foreword disclosure decision:*
*The foreword reveals the fictional ending before the reader reaches it. This reframes the cruise as dread-that-was-imagined rather than dread-that-was-real. Alternative: move the disclosure to an author's note after the text, preserving full tension through the final chapters. Both are defensible. Publishing the foreword without this disclosure — claiming the invented ending is fact — is not.*
